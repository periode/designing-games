defining games

how do you define play or game?

  • something with temporary consequences
  • a game is a series of interesting choices (quote sid meier)
  • you need to have an objective
  • fun not included
  • it cannot be easy (some games are satisfying without the hard part)
  • there is some sort of exploration (uncertainty)
  • there is some participatory aspect

a game is structured play

a series of interesting choices (short-term consequence, mid-term and long-term)

a series of self-inflicted obstacles

agon

competition

  • chess
  • sports
  • card games
  • ...

Agon means "against", and refers to the family of competitive games, where beating other players is a core aspect of the enjoyment.

The Olympic Games are perhaps one of the oldest examples of agonistic play in Western society, but competitive play as a means of resolving disputes can also be seen in knight tournaments, or ball games in pre-columbian Central America.

alea

randomness

  • poker
  • dice games
  • slot machines
  • ...

Alea, the greek word for "dice" is about luck, and its connection to the divine. The pleasure that players take from chance games has something to do with the belief that goes beyond mere probabilities. "Lady Luck" is a popular embodiment of fate personified, who bestows fate upon the chosen one. Indeed, to roll the perfect number, or pick the perfect card, at the exact time, has a feeling of magic.

acting

  • theater
  • role-playing
  • spectatorship

One of the oldest forms of play is make-believe: by pretending to be someone else, we create a whole new fictional and narrative dimension to the experience of play. Actors play for us, to allow us to suspend our disbelief, and enter in a parallel world, for a moment.

This suspension of disbelief is a core concept of game design: to let the player temporarily leave the real world behind and believe in the rules of the world you've created.

game exercise

five fingers

  1. split up in two groups
  2. play a few rounds
  3. come back and discuss

Five Fingers is a good example of how social play can emerge from simple structured rules. Once all players agree to step into the fiction of the game (fingers are lives, pointing removes a life), the logical rules transform into emotional outbursts and social politics.

The rules of games are a kind of conversational grammar‚Äîplayers use that grammar to create meaning and express themselves to each other.
